# Design Nonprofit Board Governance

Apply the specific fiduciary duties — care, loyalty, and obedience to mission — and structural practices distinct from for-profit corporate governance, since a nonprofit board answers to its mission and the public interest rather than to shareholders, rather than applying a for-profit governance template unchanged.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** BoardSource, the leading US resource organization for nonprofit governance, documents the specific fiduciary duty framework (care, loyalty, and obedience) and governance structures used across nonprofit boards nationally, and its "Nonprofit Governance Index" research is the standard reference point for evaluating nonprofit board practice against peer organizations.
**Impact:** Nonprofit boards that apply for-profit governance assumptions unchanged — for instance, assuming shareholder-style profit-maximization is the board's guiding obligation, or omitting the mission-fidelity duty distinctive to nonprofit fiduciary law — are documented to experience governance failures specific to this mismatch, including mission drift (the organization's activities gradually diverging from its stated charitable purpose) that a for-profit-style board focused purely on financial performance is less positioned to catch.
**Why best:** A nonprofit board's fiduciary duty of obedience — the obligation to ensure the organization stays true to its stated mission and legal purpose — has no direct equivalent in for-profit governance, where the board's duty runs to shareholders and financial performance; applying a for-profit template without adapting for this distinct duty leaves the organization's actual defining obligation (mission fidelity) without the board oversight it specifically requires.

## Steps

### Step 1: Establish the board's understanding of its three fiduciary duties

Ensure the board explicitly understands its three fiduciary duties — care (making informed, reasonable decisions), loyalty (acting in the organization's interest over personal interest), and obedience (ensuring the organization stays true to its mission and legal purpose) — since the duty of obedience specifically has no direct for-profit equivalent and is often the least explicitly understood by directors coming from for-profit governance backgrounds.

### Step 2: Structure board composition around mission-relevant expertise and community representation

Structure board composition to include a mix of skills genuinely relevant to the organization's specific mission and operating needs (financial oversight, legal, program-area expertise) alongside meaningful representation of the communities the organization serves, rather than composing the board purely around donor capacity or prestige.

### Step 3: Establish board oversight of mission fidelity, not just financial performance

Establish a specific mechanism for the board to evaluate whether the organization's activities remain consistent with its stated mission and legal purpose — reviewing program outcomes against mission, not only financial statements — since financial health alone doesn't confirm mission fidelity.

### Step 4: Adapt conflict-of-interest and related-party practices for the nonprofit context

Apply conflict-of-interest disclosure and related-party transaction review adapted for the nonprofit context, since nonprofit board members are frequently also donors, community members with personal ties to beneficiaries, or affiliated with partner organizations — relationships that require the same disclosure discipline as for-profit related-party review, adapted to the nonprofit's specific relationship patterns.

### Step 5: Establish CEO/executive director oversight appropriate to the nonprofit structure

Establish the board's oversight of the executive director or CEO, including performance evaluation and succession planning, adapted to the nonprofit context — where the executive director role often combines operational leadership with a public-facing mission-representation role distinct from a for-profit CEO's primarily commercial mandate.

## Rules

- Ensure the board explicitly understands all three fiduciary duties, including the duty of obedience to mission, which has no direct for-profit equivalent.
- Compose the board around mission-relevant expertise and community representation, not purely around donor capacity or prestige.
- Establish a specific mechanism for evaluating mission fidelity, not only financial performance oversight.
- Adapt conflict-of-interest and related-party practices for the nonprofit's specific relationship patterns (donors, community ties, partner organizations).

## Examples

**Mission-fidelity oversight catching program drift:** A nonprofit board's dedicated mission-fidelity review reveals that the organization's programs have gradually shifted toward activities more attractive to a major funder but increasingly distant from the organization's original stated charitable purpose. The board's duty of obedience specifically requires addressing this drift — a check a purely financial-performance-focused board (without this nonprofit-specific duty) might not have applied.

**Board composed for mission relevance, not just donor capacity:** A nonprofit board deliberately recruits members with direct program-area expertise and lived experience from the community it serves, alongside members with financial and legal expertise — rather than composing the board purely around members' capacity to make large personal donations, ensuring the board's composition genuinely serves its oversight function.

## Common Mistakes

- **Applying for-profit governance assumptions unchanged, omitting the duty of obedience to mission** — this duty has no direct for-profit equivalent and requires deliberate attention that a straight-transplanted for-profit template misses.
- **Composing the board purely around donor capacity rather than genuine mission-relevant expertise and community representation** — this can produce a board well-positioned to fundraise but poorly positioned to provide substantive programmatic and mission oversight.
- **Evaluating only financial performance without a specific mechanism for assessing mission fidelity** — financial health doesn't confirm the organization's actual activities remain consistent with its stated charitable purpose.
- **Failing to adapt conflict-of-interest practices for the nonprofit's specific relationship patterns** — nonprofit board members' ties to donors, beneficiaries, and partner organizations require disclosure discipline adapted to these particular relationship types.

## When NOT to Use

- For a for-profit corporation — this practice applies specifically to the distinct fiduciary duty structure of nonprofit governance; see `design-corporate-governance-structure` for the for-profit equivalent.
- For a very small, all-volunteer nonprofit where formal board governance infrastructure is disproportionate to the organization's current scale — apply governance rigor proportionate to organizational size and complexity.
- When the nonprofit's specific legal form (e.g., certain religious or governmental affiliate structures) carries distinct governance requirements that differ from the general nonprofit board model — confirm the specific applicable requirements for the organization's legal structure.
