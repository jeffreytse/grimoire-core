# Design Fortress Balance Sheet Strategy

Maintain capital and liquidity buffers well beyond regulatory minimums — deliberately holding more capital and liquidity than required, even at the cost of lower near-term returns on equity — so the institution can survive a severe crisis intact and, ideally, capitalize on distressed opportunities while weaker, thinner-capitalized competitors are forced to retrench.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** Jamie Dimon, CEO of JPMorgan Chase, has publicly and repeatedly articulated the "fortress balance sheet" philosophy as a core strategic principle, and JPMorgan's documented performance through the 2008 financial crisis — remaining profitable and well-capitalized while numerous other major financial institutions failed or required emergency government support — is widely cited as a direct result of maintaining substantially stronger capital and liquidity buffers than regulatory minimums required in the years preceding the crisis.
**Impact:** JPMorgan's documented position during the 2008 crisis specifically allowed it to continue normal operations, avoid the emergency capital raises and government support many competitors required, and pursue acquisitions of distressed competitors (Bear Stearns, Washington Mutual) at advantageous terms — outcomes directly attributed to having maintained a stronger capital position than regulatory minimums or industry norms required in the years before the crisis hit.
**Why best:** Maintaining capital and liquidity precisely at regulatory minimums optimizes for near-term return on equity but leaves an institution with minimal margin for error if conditions deteriorate more severely than anticipated — a fortress balance sheet accepts a lower near-term return in exchange for the resilience to survive a severe, unanticipated crisis intact, and the strategic optionality to acquire distressed competitors or assets when weaker competitors are forced to sell, an advantage unavailable to an institution simultaneously fighting for its own survival.

Sources: Documented account of Jamie Dimon and JPMorgan Chase's capital and liquidity management philosophy, particularly through the 2008 financial crisis

## Steps

### Step 1: Set capital and liquidity targets meaningfully above regulatory minimums

Establish internal capital and liquidity targets set deliberately above what regulators require — not merely compliant with minimums, but built with a margin explicitly intended to absorb a scenario more severe than regulatory stress tests assume.

### Step 2: Stress-test the balance sheet against scenarios more severe than regulatory requirements

Run internal stress tests using scenarios more severe than the standard regulatory stress-test requirements, specifically to verify the fortress-level buffers would actually hold up under a genuinely severe, low-probability crisis, not just the moderate scenarios regulatory minimums are calibrated to.

### Step 3: Accept a lower near-term return on equity as the deliberate cost of resilience

Recognize explicitly that maintaining capital and liquidity above what's required reduces near-term return on equity (excess capital and liquidity generally earn a lower return than the core business could otherwise generate) — treat this as the deliberate, accepted cost of the strategy, not an inefficiency to be optimized away.

### Step 4: Maintain the discipline through periods when it appears unnecessary

Sustain the fortress-level buffers even during extended periods of benign conditions when competitors maintaining thinner buffers may show higher near-term returns — the strategy's value is specifically realized during severe, infrequent crisis periods, and abandoning the discipline during a long calm period defeats its purpose.

### Step 5: Position to act opportunistically when weaker competitors are forced to retrench

Maintain the capacity and readiness to act opportunistically — acquiring distressed competitors, assets, or market share — specifically during a crisis period when weaker, thinner-capitalized competitors are forced to sell or retreat, since this optionality is a significant part of the fortress balance sheet's strategic value beyond mere survival.

## Rules

- Set internal capital and liquidity targets meaningfully above regulatory minimums, not merely at the compliant threshold.
- Stress-test against scenarios more severe than standard regulatory requirements to verify the buffers would genuinely hold under an extreme crisis.
- Accept lower near-term return on equity as the deliberate, accepted cost of this strategy, not something to optimize away during calm periods.
- Maintain the discipline consistently, including during extended benign periods when it may appear unnecessary relative to more aggressively-capitalized competitors.

## Examples

**Fortress balance sheet strategy validated in crisis:** A financial institution maintains capital and liquidity buffers substantially above regulatory minimums for years, accepting a lower return on equity than more aggressively-capitalized competitors during calm conditions. When a severe financial crisis hits, the institution remains well-capitalized and able to continue normal operations, while several thinner-capitalized competitors require emergency support or fail outright — and the well-capitalized institution is able to acquire distressed competitors' assets on favorable terms, realizing significant strategic value from the discipline maintained through the preceding calm period.

**Thin capitalization exposed by crisis (contrast case, illustrative):** A different institution maintains capital and liquidity closer to regulatory minimums, optimizing for higher near-term return on equity during calm conditions. When the same severe crisis hits, this institution lacks sufficient buffer to absorb the losses and liquidity strain, requiring emergency capital or support — illustrating the risk the fortress balance sheet strategy is specifically designed to avoid.

## Common Mistakes

- **Treating regulatory minimums as the appropriate target rather than a floor** — regulatory minimums are calibrated to moderate stress scenarios; a fortress strategy requires buffers well beyond this floor to survive genuinely severe, low-probability crises.
- **Abandoning the discipline during extended calm periods due to near-term return pressure** — the strategy's value is realized specifically during infrequent severe crises; abandoning it during calm periods defeats its purpose.
- **Stress-testing only against standard regulatory scenarios** — genuine fortress-level resilience requires testing against more severe scenarios than regulatory minimums assume.
- **Failing to position for opportunistic action during a crisis** — maintaining survival-level buffers without also preparing to act opportunistically on distressed opportunities forgoes a significant part of the strategy's potential value.

## When NOT to Use

- For a company or institution where the cost of maintaining excess capital and liquidity buffers is prohibitive relative to its actual risk profile or business model — this strategy specifically suits capital-intensive, systemically-important, or crisis-exposed institutions where survival through a severe downturn carries outsized strategic value.
- When near-term competitive pressure genuinely requires matching thinner-capitalized competitors' returns to survive in the near term — the strategy assumes the institution can sustain a lower near-term return long enough for the resilience benefit to be realized.
- For an early-stage or non-financial company where "balance sheet fortress" concepts don't map directly — see `apply-leverage-avoidance` and `audit-balance-sheet-debt-risk` for related but distinct capital-structure risk considerations more applicable outside large financial institutions.
