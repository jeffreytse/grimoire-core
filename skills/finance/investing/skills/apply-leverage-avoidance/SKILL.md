# Apply Leverage Avoidance

Avoid margin debt and other borrowed capital in equity investing, regardless of how attractive the return amplification appears — because leverage introduces a forced-liquidation risk that can convert a temporary, survivable paper loss into a permanent, realized one, independent of whether the original investment thesis was correct.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** Warren Buffett has repeatedly and explicitly warned against investment leverage across decades of Berkshire Hathaway shareholder letters, and has stated that more investors have been financially ruined by borrowed money than by any specific analytical or valuation mistake. Berkshire itself, while a large and financially sophisticated enterprise, has historically avoided using margin debt or significant leverage in its own equity investment activities, relying instead on insurance float (a structurally different, non-callable source of investable capital) rather than borrowed money subject to margin calls.
**Impact:** The specific danger of margin leverage is not that it amplifies losses proportionally (a risk any leveraged position shares) — it's that a margin call can force liquidation of a position at the worst possible moment, during a price decline, regardless of whether the underlying business's long-term economics remain sound. This converts a temporary, survivable paper loss (one a patient, unleveraged holder could simply wait out) into a permanent, realized loss at exactly the wrong time, purely because of the leverage structure rather than any error in the original investment thesis.
**Why best:** An unleveraged investor who correctly judges a business's long-term economics can hold through a temporary decline indefinitely, waiting for the market to recognize the business's value — the single most powerful tool available to a patient, quality-focused investor (see `apply-buy-and-hold-strategy`). Leverage removes exactly this option: a margin call doesn't ask whether the thesis is still sound, it demands liquidation on a timeline set by the lender and the market, not by the investor's own analysis.

Sources: Berkshire Hathaway Shareholder Letters (Buffett, various years, berkshirehathaway.com)

## Steps

### Step 1: Recognize the specific mechanism of leverage risk, not just its magnitude

Understand that margin leverage's danger isn't merely "bigger losses if wrong" — an unleveraged investor who is wrong about a thesis also loses money. The specific, additional danger is forced liquidation on the lender's timeline during a decline, regardless of whether the thesis is ultimately right or wrong, which removes the investor's ability to simply wait out a temporary decline.

### Step 2: Avoid margin debt for equity positions as a default policy, not a case-by-case judgment call

Treat leverage avoidance as a standing rule rather than something re-evaluated favorably whenever a particular opportunity looks compelling enough to justify borrowing — the moments when leverage looks most attractive (high conviction, seemingly limited downside) are exactly the moments overconfidence is most likely to be underestimating real risk.

### Step 3: Distinguish personal margin leverage from a business's own operating leverage or float-based capital

Recognize that avoiding investor-level margin debt is a distinct question from a business's own use of debt in its operations, or a specialized capital structure like insurance float — the risk this skill addresses is specifically borrowed capital used by an individual investor to increase an equity position, which carries the callable, forced-liquidation risk that a business's own non-callable capital structures typically don't share in the same way.

### Step 4: Verify that any borrowing for other purposes doesn't functionally create equity leverage

Check that debt taken on for other stated purposes (a home equity line, a personal loan) isn't functionally being used to fund equity purchases — the forced-liquidation risk mechanism applies regardless of how the borrowed capital is labeled, if it's ultimately deployed into leveraged equity exposure.

### Step 5: If leverage exists for any reason, size positions assuming forced liquidation is possible

In the rare case where some form of leverage is unavoidable or already in place, size equity positions conservatively enough that a plausible decline wouldn't trigger forced liquidation — treating the possibility of a margin call as a real constraint on position sizing, not a remote scenario to be ignored.

## Rules

- Treat leverage avoidance as a standing policy for equity investing, not a case-by-case decision reconsidered whenever an opportunity seems compelling enough.
- Recognize the specific mechanism at risk — forced liquidation on the lender's timeline, not the investor's — as distinct from ordinary unleveraged loss.
- Distinguish personal margin debt from a business's own capital structure or float-based financing — this skill addresses the former specifically.
- If any leverage exists, size positions assuming a margin call is a real possibility, not a remote tail risk to be dismissed.

## Examples

**Leverage risk realized (illustrative failure case):** An investor uses margin debt to increase a position in a business they've researched carefully and believe is undervalued. A broad market decline (unrelated to the business's fundamentals) triggers a margin call, forcing liquidation of the position at a depressed price. Months later, the market and the specific business recover fully — but the investor no longer holds the position, having been forced to sell at the worst possible moment regardless of being correct about the underlying business.

**Avoiding leverage preserves optionality:** A different investor, holding the same conviction in the same business but without margin debt, experiences the identical decline. Because no lender can force a sale, they simply hold the position through the decline and participate fully in the subsequent recovery — the entire difference in outcome between the two investors is the presence or absence of leverage, not the quality of their original analysis.

## Common Mistakes

- **Treating leverage as a case-by-case decision rather than a standing policy** — re-evaluating leverage favorably whenever an opportunity seems compelling enough to justify it defeats the purpose of avoiding it, since high conviction is exactly when overconfidence is most likely to be underestimating real risk.
- **Underestimating how a margin call removes the option to simply wait** — the core risk isn't "losing more if wrong," it's losing the ability to hold through a temporary decline at all, regardless of whether the thesis was ultimately correct.
- **Using debt for another stated purpose while functionally leveraging equity exposure** — the forced-liquidation mechanism doesn't care how the borrowed capital is labeled if it ends up funding leveraged equity positions.
- **Conflating investor margin leverage with a business's own operating debt or float** — these are structurally different forms of leverage with very different forced-liquidation risk profiles; treating them as equivalent misapplies the caution appropriately reserved for margin debt specifically.

## When NOT to Use

- When evaluating a business's own use of debt in its operations as part of fundamental analysis — that's a distinct question (part of assessing the business itself) from whether an investor should personally use margin debt to buy the stock.
- For genuinely non-callable financing structures that don't carry forced-liquidation risk on a lender's timeline — the specific mechanism this skill warns against is callable margin debt, not every form of leverage in every context.
- When the "leverage" in question is a mortgage on a primary residence rather than borrowed capital deployed into equity positions — a different risk category with different considerations, outside this skill's scope.
