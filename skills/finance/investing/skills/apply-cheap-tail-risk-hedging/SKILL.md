# Apply Cheap Tail-Risk Hedging

Buy asymmetric, deeply out-of-the-money protection — credit default swaps, far-out-of-the-money put options, or similar instruments — against a low-probability but potentially catastrophic portfolio risk while that protection is cheap, specifically because the broader market is currently complacent about the risk. A small, clearly defined cost caps the portfolio's downside if the tail risk actually materializes, and the hedge itself can produce an outsized return precisely because it was purchased at a price reflecting the market's underestimation of the risk.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** Bill Ackman, founder of Pershing Square Capital Management, is documented for purchasing a portfolio-wide credit hedge in early 2020 for a premium of approximately $27 million, structured as protection against a broad credit-market deterioration — a tail risk the market was, at that moment, pricing as unlikely. When the COVID-19 pandemic triggered a severe market and credit dislocation shortly after, the hedge is documented as having returned approximately $2.6 billion, which Ackman then used in part to fund purchases of the fund's core long positions at distressed prices during the crash.
**Impact:** The documented outcome specifically illustrates the payoff structure this practice targets: a small, defined-cost premium (a tiny fraction of total assets under management) that, in the tail scenario the market was underpricing, returned roughly 100 times its cost — and the proceeds were specifically redeployed to buy the fund's core long positions at the depressed prices the same crisis created, turning portfolio insurance into a source of crisis-period buying power rather than merely a loss-limiting instrument.
**Why best:** Broad portfolio de-risking (selling positions or moving to cash ahead of a feared downturn) sacrifices upside if the feared event doesn't occur, and is difficult to time correctly. A cheap, asymmetric tail hedge instead caps the defined cost of being wrong about the risk (the premium paid) while preserving full participation in the core long portfolio if the risk doesn't materialize — and specifically pays off in exactly the scenario (a severe, sudden dislocation) when having available capital to deploy is most valuable.

## Steps

### Step 1: Identify a low-probability, high-impact risk the portfolio is exposed to

Identify a specific tail risk — a broad credit-market deterioration, a systemic market dislocation, or another low-probability but potentially severe scenario — that would meaningfully damage the portfolio's core holdings if it occurred, distinct from the ordinary volatility the portfolio is otherwise prepared to weather.

### Step 2: Find an instrument priced cheaply because the market is currently complacent about that risk

Look for a hedging instrument — credit default swap protection, far out-of-the-money put options, or a similar asymmetric structure — priced cheaply specifically because current market sentiment assigns the tail risk a low probability, since the entire economics of the trade depend on acquiring the protection before the market reprices the risk higher.

### Step 3: Size the hedge premium as a small, clearly defined percentage of total assets

Size the cost of the hedge as a small, clearly bounded percentage of total portfolio assets — the position's maximum possible loss is simply the premium paid, and this cost must be small enough to sustain indefinitely if the tail risk never materializes, since the hedge may expire worthless in the majority of scenarios where the feared event doesn't occur.

### Step 4: Hold the hedge through ordinary volatility without early unwinding

Hold the hedge through ordinary market fluctuations without prematurely unwinding it for a small gain or to avoid the recurring cost of maintaining it — the hedge's value is realized specifically in the tail scenario, and exiting early to avoid the carrying cost defeats its purpose.

### Step 5: Deploy hedge proceeds opportunistically if the tail risk materializes

If the tail risk materializes and the hedge pays off, consider redeploying some or all of the proceeds into the portfolio's core long positions at the distressed prices the same crisis likely created — realizing the hedge's full strategic value not just as a loss-limiter, but as a source of crisis-period buying power.

## Rules

- Acquire tail-risk protection while the market is complacent and pricing it cheaply — the trade's asymmetric payoff depends specifically on this mispricing.
- Size the hedge premium as a small, sustainable percentage of total assets, since the hedge will often expire worthless if the tail risk doesn't occur.
- Hold the hedge through ordinary volatility without prematurely unwinding it to avoid its carrying cost.
- Consider redeploying hedge proceeds into core long positions at distressed prices if the tail risk materializes, rather than treating the payoff as merely a loss offset.

## Examples

**Tail hedge paying off during a genuine dislocation:** A portfolio manager purchases broad credit protection for a small premium while the market is pricing credit risk as low. A subsequent severe, unexpected market dislocation causes credit spreads to widen sharply, and the hedge pays out many multiples of its original cost — proceeds are then used to purchase core long positions at prices depressed by the same dislocation, compounding the hedge's strategic value beyond simple loss protection.

**Hedge expiring worthless as an accepted, budgeted cost:** A different portfolio maintains a similar tail hedge for an extended period during which no severe dislocation occurs, and the hedge expires worthless, costing its full premium. This is a normal, expected outcome of the strategy in the majority of scenarios — the small, budgeted cost is the deliberate price paid for protection against the minority of scenarios where the tail risk does occur.

## Common Mistakes

- **Waiting to buy protection until after the market has already begun pricing the risk higher** — the asymmetric payoff specifically depends on acquiring the hedge while the market remains complacent; buying after sentiment shifts is far more expensive and less asymmetric.
- **Sizing the hedge premium too large relative to total assets** — the hedge is expected to expire worthless in most scenarios, so its recurring cost must be small enough to sustain indefinitely without meaningfully dragging on overall portfolio returns.
- **Unwinding the hedge prematurely to avoid its carrying cost** — this forfeits the payoff in exactly the tail scenario the hedge was purchased to protect against.
- **Treating hedge proceeds solely as a loss offset rather than considering opportunistic redeployment** — the crisis that triggers the hedge's payoff often simultaneously creates distressed buying opportunities in the portfolio's core holdings.

## When NOT to Use

- Without access to genuinely liquid, appropriately structured hedging instruments (credit default swaps, listed far-out-of-the-money options) for the specific tail risk being targeted — an improperly structured or illiquid hedge may not deliver the intended asymmetric payoff when needed.
- For a portfolio where even the small, defined hedge premium is not sustainable given available capital — the strategy specifically requires being able to absorb a recurring cost that may produce no payoff for an extended period.
- As a substitute for genuine fundamental risk management of the core portfolio — a tail hedge protects against a specific systemic scenario, not poor-quality individual positions within the core portfolio (see `apply-downside-protection-principle` for that separate discipline).
