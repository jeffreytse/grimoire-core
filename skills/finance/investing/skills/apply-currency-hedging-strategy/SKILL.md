# Apply Currency Hedging Strategy

When holding assets or expecting cash flows denominated in a foreign currency, deliberately decide whether and how much of the resulting currency exposure to hedge — using forwards, futures, or options — so that currency movements don't dominate or obscure the return of the underlying investment or business activity the currency exposure is merely incidental to.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** Currency hedging of foreign-denominated assets and cash flows is standard practice across institutional asset management and corporate treasury functions, documented in the CFA Institute's investment management curriculum as a core component of international portfolio and corporate risk management, and routinely implemented by multinational corporations and global asset managers to manage foreign-exchange exposure arising from cross-border investments and operations.
**Impact:** An unhedged foreign-currency position adds currency volatility on top of the underlying asset's own volatility — for many foreign investments, currency movements can be as large as or larger than the underlying asset's own price movement over a given period, meaning an unhedged investor's realized return can be dominated by currency fluctuation unrelated to the underlying investment thesis being right or wrong.
**Why best:** Failing to consider currency exposure separately from the underlying investment conflates two genuinely distinct sources of risk and return — the underlying asset's performance and the currency's movement — and an investor or business that hasn't deliberately decided how much of the currency component to hedge is implicitly taking a large, often unintended currency bet alongside the intended underlying investment or business decision.

## Steps

### Step 1: Identify and quantify the underlying foreign-currency exposure

Identify the specific foreign-currency exposure arising from a holding or expected cash flow — the currency, the amount, and the timing — as the starting point for any hedging decision, since an imprecise understanding of the actual exposure undermines any subsequent hedge sizing.

### Step 2: Decide the target hedge ratio based on the exposure's purpose

Decide what fraction of the currency exposure to hedge (the hedge ratio) based on the purpose of the underlying position — a exposure held for a specific, near-term, currency-sensitive purpose (e.g., funding a known future foreign-currency liability) often warrants a higher hedge ratio than a long-term equity position where currency exposure may be an accepted, deliberate part of the diversification thesis.

### Step 3: Select an appropriate hedging instrument for the exposure's characteristics

Select a hedging instrument — a forward contract (fixing an exchange rate for a future date), a futures contract (an exchange-traded equivalent), or an option (providing asymmetric protection at the cost of a premium) — based on the exposure's certainty of timing and amount, and whether asymmetric (option-like) or symmetric (forward-like) protection better fits the underlying position's needs.

### Step 4: Size and execute the hedge to match the target hedge ratio

Size the hedging instrument's notional amount to match the decided target hedge ratio against the underlying exposure, and execute the hedge — recognizing that an improperly sized hedge (too small or too large relative to the actual exposure) can leave residual currency risk or even introduce a new, unintended currency exposure in the opposite direction.

### Step 5: Monitor and roll or adjust the hedge as the underlying exposure changes

Monitor the hedge against the underlying exposure over time, rolling forward contracts as they approach expiration and adjusting the hedge ratio or size if the underlying exposure's amount, timing, or purpose changes — a hedge sized correctly at inception can become mismatched if the underlying position changes without a corresponding hedge adjustment.

## Rules

- Quantify the specific currency, amount, and timing of the underlying exposure precisely before sizing any hedge.
- Set the hedge ratio deliberately based on the underlying position's purpose, not as a default of either fully hedging or not hedging at all.
- Match the hedging instrument (forward, futures, option) to the exposure's certainty of timing and amount, and to whether symmetric or asymmetric protection is more appropriate.
- Monitor and adjust the hedge over time as the underlying exposure changes — a hedge accurate at inception can become mismatched without ongoing adjustment.

## Examples

**Currency hedge isolating the underlying investment thesis:** An investor holds a foreign-equity position specifically for its business fundamentals, and hedges a substantial portion of the resulting currency exposure using forward contracts, so that the position's realized return primarily reflects the underlying business's performance rather than being dominated by currency fluctuation unrelated to that thesis.

**Deliberately unhedged currency exposure as an accepted diversification choice:** A different investor holds a long-term international equity allocation and deliberately chooses not to hedge the currency exposure, treating currency diversification as an intentional, accepted component of the broader portfolio's diversification strategy — a legitimate choice as long as it is made deliberately, not by default inattention to the exposure.

## Common Mistakes

- **Failing to quantify the actual currency exposure before deciding on a hedge** — an imprecise understanding of the exposure's amount and timing undermines any subsequent hedge sizing.
- **Applying a default hedge ratio (fully hedged or fully unhedged) without considering the underlying position's specific purpose** — the appropriate hedge ratio depends on what the underlying exposure is for, not a one-size-fits-all default.
- **Sizing a hedge that doesn't match the actual underlying exposure amount** — an oversized or undersized hedge can leave residual risk or introduce a new, unintended currency exposure in the opposite direction.
- **Setting a hedge once and failing to monitor or adjust it as the underlying exposure changes over time** — a hedge accurate at inception can become significantly mismatched without ongoing review.

## When NOT to Use

- For a currency exposure explicitly and deliberately accepted as part of a diversification thesis — hedging away currency exposure an investor deliberately wants defeats the purpose of holding it.
- When the cost of hedging (bid-ask spread, option premium, or opportunity cost) is disproportionate to the size or duration of the underlying exposure being protected.
- For a purely domestic portfolio with no genuine foreign-currency exposure — this practice applies specifically to holdings or cash flows denominated in a foreign currency relative to the investor's base currency.
