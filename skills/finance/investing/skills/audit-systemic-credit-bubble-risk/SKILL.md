# Audit Systemic Credit Bubble Risk

Verify the underlying quality of loans or assets directly, rather than trusting credit ratings or broad market consensus, when assessing whether an entire asset class or credit market is systemically mispriced — since ratings agencies and market consensus can be structurally wrong across an entire market simultaneously, not just for one issuer.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** John Paulson and Paulson & Co. are widely documented for extensive research conducted before 2007 directly examining the underlying quality of loans within the U.S. subprime mortgage market — rather than relying on the high credit ratings assigned to mortgage-backed securities built from those loans — leading to a position against the market that became one of the most widely documented profitable trades in financial history once the underlying loan quality proved as weak as the direct research had indicated.
**Impact:** The documented pre-2007 research specifically found that underlying loan quality (borrower creditworthiness, loan-to-value ratios, underwriting standards) had deteriorated substantially in ways not reflected in the high credit ratings assigned to the securities built from those loans — demonstrating that ratings and broad market consensus can diverge substantially from the actual, directly-verifiable quality of the underlying assets across an entire market simultaneously, not merely for isolated issuers.
**Why best:** Credit ratings and market consensus are themselves subject to systemic error — modeling assumptions, incentive structures at ratings agencies, and a broad market narrative can all be wrong in the same direction simultaneously across an entire asset class, especially during a period of rapid credit expansion. Verifying underlying asset quality directly, rather than relying on ratings or consensus as a substitute for that verification, is the only way to detect a systemic mispricing before it becomes broadly recognized.

Sources: Documented accounts of John Paulson and Paulson & Co.'s pre-2007 subprime mortgage market research and positioning

## Steps

### Step 1: Identify a candidate systemic risk — rapid credit expansion with weakening standards

Look for asset classes or credit markets experiencing rapid growth in volume alongside signs of weakening underwriting standards — expanding credit availability to increasingly marginal borrowers or assets, a pattern historically associated with systemic mispricing risk.

### Step 2: Verify underlying asset/loan quality directly, not through ratings alone

Investigate the actual underlying loans or assets directly — borrower creditworthiness, collateral quality, loan-to-value ratios, and underwriting documentation — rather than relying on the credit rating assigned to securities built from those assets as a substitute for this direct verification.

### Step 3: Assess whether ratings or consensus pricing reflect the actual verified quality

Compare the direct findings about underlying asset quality against how the market is pricing the related securities and what rating they've been assigned — a substantial gap between directly-verified quality and market pricing/ratings is the specific signal this analysis targets.

### Step 4: Identify an appropriate instrument to express the thesis

Where a genuine systemic mispricing is identified, assess what specific financial instruments (direct short positions, credit default swaps, or other derivatives) are available to express the thesis, and understand the specific risk and cost structure of whichever instrument is used — some instruments carry ongoing costs or specific counterparty risks that must be weighed against the potential payoff.

### Step 5: Size the position accounting for the timing uncertainty of systemic corrections

Recognize that a systemic mispricing, even when correctly identified, may take considerably longer to correct than an individual mispricing — broad consensus and structural momentum can sustain a mispriced market for an extended period — and size the position accounting for the cost of being early, not just the eventual payoff if the thesis proves correct.

## Rules

- Verify underlying asset or loan quality directly — never treat a credit rating or market consensus as a substitute for this direct verification.
- Look specifically for rapid credit expansion combined with weakening underwriting standards as the candidate pattern for systemic risk.
- Assess the specific gap between directly-verified quality and how the market is currently pricing the related securities.
- Size positions accounting for the real possibility that a correctly-identified systemic mispricing takes longer to correct than expected.

## Examples

**Systemic mispricing correctly identified:** An investor conducts direct research into the underlying loan quality within a rapidly-growing segment of a credit market, finding underwriting standards have deteriorated substantially in ways not reflected in the high credit ratings assigned to the related securities. Identifying this gap between verified underlying quality and market pricing, the investor takes a position sized to account for the real possibility that broad market consensus sustains the mispricing for an extended period before it eventually corrects.

**Relying on ratings without direct verification (the failure mode this avoids):** A different market participant relies on the high credit ratings assigned to securities in the same market, without directly verifying the underlying loan quality, and continues holding exposure to the related securities as the underlying quality deteriorates further — illustrating the specific risk of trusting ratings and consensus as a substitute for direct verification.

## Common Mistakes

- **Trusting credit ratings as a substitute for direct verification of underlying asset quality** — ratings can be systematically wrong across an entire market, particularly during periods of rapid credit expansion.
- **Underestimating how long a correctly-identified systemic mispricing can persist before correcting** — broad consensus and structural momentum can sustain a mispriced market considerably longer than an individual investor's timeline might assume.
- **Choosing an instrument to express the thesis without understanding its specific cost and risk structure** — some instruments carry ongoing costs or counterparty risks that must be weighed carefully against the potential payoff.
- **Applying this analysis without genuine direct verification of underlying assets** — a vague suspicion of a bubble without the underlying research to support it is speculation, not the systemic analysis this approach requires.

## When NOT to Use

- Without the ability or resources to genuinely verify underlying asset quality directly — this approach specifically depends on direct verification, not inference from market sentiment alone.
- For an isolated, single-issuer mispricing rather than a genuinely systemic, market-wide one — see `apply-forensic-accounting-short-thesis` for the individual-company analog to this systemic-level analysis.
- When the specific instruments needed to express a systemic short thesis carry costs or risks that make the position impractical for the available capital and risk tolerance.
