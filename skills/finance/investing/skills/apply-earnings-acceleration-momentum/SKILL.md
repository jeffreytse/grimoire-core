# Apply Earnings Acceleration Momentum

Favor companies whose earnings growth rate is itself accelerating — growing faster than the prior period's growth rate — rather than companies with a high but stable or decelerating growth rate, since acceleration in the growth rate itself is a distinct and often more powerful signal than the absolute growth rate alone.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** Foster Friess built a documented growth-investing career specifically emphasizing earnings acceleration — the rate of change in a company's earnings growth rate — as a primary screening criterion, distinct from simply screening for companies with a high absolute growth rate, becoming known for consistently applying this specific momentum-based lens to growth-stock selection.
**Impact:** A company growing earnings at a high but stable or decelerating rate is a fundamentally different situation from one whose growth rate is itself accelerating — the latter often signals an inflection point (a new product gaining traction, a business model reaching a scaling threshold, or a market shifting favorably) that the market has not yet fully priced in, since market participants and analysts tend to extrapolate recent trends rather than anticipate a genuine acceleration.
**Why best:** Screening only for a high absolute growth rate treats a company with stable, well-recognized 20% growth the same as one whose growth rate has just accelerated from 10% to 25% — but the second case represents a meaningful change in trajectory that the market, anchored to the prior trend, may not have fully incorporated into the price yet, providing a distinct opportunity that a static growth-rate screen alone would miss.

Sources: Documented account of Foster Friess's growth-investing approach and public commentary on earnings acceleration

## Steps

### Step 1: Track the earnings growth rate across multiple recent periods, not just the most recent one

Calculate the earnings growth rate for several consecutive recent periods (quarters or years) to establish a trend in the growth rate itself, not just a single period's figure — the signal this approach targets is the trajectory of the growth rate, which requires multiple data points to establish.

### Step 2: Identify genuine acceleration, not a one-time anomaly

Confirm the growth rate has genuinely accelerated across at least two consecutive periods, and investigate the underlying driver of the acceleration (a new product, a scaling business model, a favorable market shift) — a single period's unusually high growth figure driven by a one-time factor is a weaker signal than a genuine, multi-period accelerating trend with an identifiable driver.

### Step 3: Assess whether the market has already priced in the acceleration

Check current valuation and analyst expectations to assess whether the acceleration is already broadly recognized and priced in, or whether it remains under-appreciated — the opportunity this approach targets is specifically in the gap between a genuine, underlying acceleration and a market that hasn't yet fully adjusted its expectations to reflect it.

### Step 4: Verify the accelerating growth isn't coming at the cost of deteriorating fundamentals elsewhere

Cross-check that the accelerating earnings growth isn't accompanied by warning signs elsewhere — deteriorating margins, rising inventory relative to sales (see `audit-inventory-growth-red-flag`), or aggressive revenue recognition — that would suggest the reported acceleration doesn't reflect genuine, sustainable underlying momentum.

### Step 5: Monitor for deceleration as the exit signal

Since this approach specifically targets accelerating growth, treat a subsequent deceleration in the growth rate — even if the absolute growth rate remains reasonably high — as a meaningful signal warranting re-assessment, since the specific momentum-based thesis depended on continued acceleration, not simply continued high absolute growth.

## Rules

- Track the growth rate trend across multiple periods, not a single period's figure, before concluding genuine acceleration is present.
- Require an identifiable underlying driver for the acceleration, not just a favorable but unexplained data point.
- Verify accelerating earnings aren't accompanied by other fundamental warning signs that would undermine the sustainability of the reported growth.
- Treat subsequent deceleration as a meaningful exit signal, since the thesis specifically depends on continued acceleration, not just continued growth.

## Examples

**Genuine acceleration identified and acted on:** An investor tracks a company's earnings growth rate across several recent quarters, finding it has accelerated meaningfully from a stable historical rate, driven by an identifiable underlying cause (a new product line gaining rapid traction). Checking that current valuation and analyst expectations haven't yet fully incorporated this acceleration, and confirming no accompanying fundamental warning signs, the investor takes a position specifically on this momentum thesis.

**High but non-accelerating growth correctly distinguished:** A different company shows a high and stable growth rate that has been consistent for many periods without acceleration. Recognizing this as a fundamentally different situation from genuine acceleration — likely already well-recognized and priced in by the market given its stability and duration — the investor doesn't treat it as fitting this specific momentum-based thesis, even though the absolute growth rate is comparably high.

## Common Mistakes

- **Screening for high absolute growth rate without checking whether it's accelerating** — a stable high growth rate is a fundamentally different signal from a genuinely accelerating one, and the market may have already priced in the former.
- **Treating a single period's unusually high growth figure as genuine acceleration** — confirm the trend across multiple periods and identify an underlying driver before concluding genuine acceleration is present.
- **Ignoring accompanying fundamental warning signs** — verify the accelerating earnings growth isn't coming alongside deteriorating margins or aggressive accounting that would undermine its sustainability.
- **Holding through a subsequent deceleration as if the original thesis were unaffected** — since the thesis specifically depends on continued acceleration, a deceleration is a meaningful signal for re-assessment, not something to be ignored because absolute growth remains positive.

## When NOT to Use

- For a stable, mature stalwart or slow grower (see `apply-stock-categorization-framework`) where accelerating growth isn't a relevant or expected characteristic.
- When no reliable multi-period growth-rate data exists to establish a genuine trend, as opposed to a single favorable data point.
- As a substitute for full fundamental and valuation analysis — see `calculate-peg-ratio` and standard growth-stock due diligence for the complementary analysis this momentum-focused screen doesn't replace.
